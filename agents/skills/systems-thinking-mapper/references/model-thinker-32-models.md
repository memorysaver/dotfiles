# The Model Thinker: 32 Model Lenses

Use this reference when the user asks which model to use, mentions Scott E. Page or *The Model Thinker*, or wants a many-model portfolio. This is an original working index aligned with the book's public table of contents and many-model paradigm; it is not a substitute for the book and does not reproduce the book's explanations.

Public sources used for orientation: Scott E. Page's site describes the book as a many-model approach using ensembles of models; public library and bookstore records list chapters such as normal distributions, power-law distributions, linear models, networks, diffusion, entropy, random walks, path dependence, Markov models, systems dynamics, threshold models, game theory, mechanism design, signaling, learning, bandits, and rugged landscapes.

## How to use this index

Use these lenses to expand, challenge, or complement a systems-thinking diagram.

For most user problems:

1. Start with a systems model if the problem is recurring over time.
2. Pick 2-5 model lenses from this index.
3. Apply each lens using the standard format in `model-thinking.md`.
4. Synthesize convergent insights, tensions, robust actions, and next evidence.

## Standard lens format

Each model lens below uses the same fields:

- **Use when**: where the model is most useful.
- **Core question**: the question it helps answer.
- **Watch for**: signals that the lens fits.
- **Blind spot**: how it can mislead.

## Quick selection guide

- Repeated worsening, delays, workarounds: systems dynamics, threshold feedback, path dependence.
- Extreme outcomes or rare large events: power-law, entropy, random walk.
- Prediction from measured variables: linear model, normal distribution.
- Social spread or adoption: broadcast, diffusion/contagion, network, threshold.
- Strategic behavior: game theory, cooperation, collective action, mechanism design, signaling.
- Adaptation and exploration: learning, multi-armed bandit, rugged landscape.
- Place, taste, and matching: spatial choice, hedonic choice.
- Lock-in and history: path dependence, local interaction, Markov.

## The 32 lenses

### 1. Categorization and Bias-Variance

- **Use when**: a problem depends on how cases are grouped, classified, scored, or segmented.
- **Core question**: Are we using categories that are too coarse, too fine, or based on the wrong attributes?
- **Watch for**: personas, risk buckets, labels, customer segments, evaluation rubrics.
- **Blind spot**: categories can look precise while hiding variation inside each group.

### 2. Rational Actor

- **Use when**: actors have clear goals, incentives, options, and constraints.
- **Core question**: What would an actor choose if they optimized for their objective?
- **Watch for**: pricing, contracts, resource allocation, strategic trade-offs.
- **Blind spot**: people may be biased, habitual, social, confused, or operating with limited information.

### 3. Behavioral Actor

- **Use when**: cognitive limits, biases, emotions, salience, or framing likely shape behavior.
- **Core question**: How might real human judgment differ from optimization?
- **Watch for**: overreaction, loss aversion, status quo bias, short-termism, anchoring.
- **Blind spot**: bias explanations can become vague if not tied to observable behavior.

### 4. Rule-Based Actor

- **Use when**: people or organizations follow routines, heuristics, policies, or habits.
- **Core question**: What simple rule is producing the observed pattern?
- **Watch for**: playbooks, approval rules, escalation paths, standard operating procedures.
- **Blind spot**: rules may change once actors learn the system is being modeled.

### 5. Normal Distribution

- **Use when**: outcomes arise from many small independent additive factors.
- **Core question**: Is variation clustered around an average with rare symmetric extremes?
- **Watch for**: test scores, measurement error, aggregate averages, process variation.
- **Blind spot**: extreme events may be more common than a normal model predicts.

### 6. Power-Law Distribution

- **Use when**: a few outcomes dominate many small outcomes.
- **Core question**: Are we in a long-tail world where averages hide concentration?
- **Watch for**: city sizes, wealth, popularity, outages, sales hits, network hubs.
- **Blind spot**: not every unequal distribution is a power law.

### 7. Linear Model

- **Use when**: you need a clear first approximation of how inputs relate to an outcome.
- **Core question**: How much does the outcome change when one variable changes?
- **Watch for**: regression, scoring, forecasting, performance drivers.
- **Blind spot**: linear models can miss thresholds, interactions, feedback, and nonlinear returns.

### 8. Concavity and Convexity

- **Use when**: returns change as scale, effort, or risk increases.
- **Core question**: Are marginal gains shrinking, growing, or changing sign?
- **Watch for**: diminishing returns, compounding upside, fragility, risk exposure.
- **Blind spot**: the curve shape is often assumed rather than measured.

### 9. Value and Power

- **Use when**: you need to allocate credit, influence, bargaining power, or contribution.
- **Core question**: Who creates value, who captures value, and why?
- **Watch for**: coalitions, platform ecosystems, teams, bottleneck roles.
- **Blind spot**: formal allocations can ignore legitimacy, fairness, and hidden labor.

### 10. Network Model

- **Use when**: connections among actors shape outcomes.
- **Core question**: How does network structure affect information, influence, risk, or power?
- **Watch for**: hubs, bridges, clusters, bottlenecks, contagion paths.
- **Blind spot**: a static network may miss changing relationships and adaptive behavior.

### 11. Broadcast Model

- **Use when**: one source sends information or influence to many recipients.
- **Core question**: How far does a message reach from a central sender?
- **Watch for**: announcements, media campaigns, executive communication, alerts.
- **Blind spot**: broadcast reach does not guarantee adoption, trust, or behavior change.

### 12. Diffusion and Contagion

- **Use when**: adoption, behavior, disease, information, or risk spreads through contact.
- **Core question**: What causes spread to accelerate, stall, or saturate?
- **Watch for**: adoption curves, virality, epidemics, rumors, social proof.
- **Blind spot**: diffusion models can underplay individual choice and institutional constraints.

### 13. Entropy and Uncertainty

- **Use when**: the key issue is uncertainty, surprise, information, or disorder.
- **Core question**: How uncertain is the system, and what information would reduce uncertainty?
- **Watch for**: ambiguous forecasts, noisy signals, missing priors, scenario spread.
- **Blind spot**: information-theoretic clarity does not automatically produce action.

### 14. Random Walk

- **Use when**: changes accumulate from many random steps.
- **Core question**: Could the observed path be drift rather than meaningful trend?
- **Watch for**: prices, performance streaks, wandering metrics, noisy time series.
- **Blind spot**: random-walk assumptions can miss structural breaks and feedback.

### 15. Path Dependence

- **Use when**: early events shape later options and lock-in.
- **Core question**: How did history constrain the current path?
- **Watch for**: standards, habits, installed base, sunk costs, institutional memory.
- **Blind spot**: path dependence can become a story that excuses current choices.

### 16. Local Interaction

- **Use when**: global patterns emerge from nearby interactions.
- **Core question**: Can simple local rules produce the large-scale pattern?
- **Watch for**: segregation, peer effects, clustering, norms, neighborhood effects.
- **Blind spot**: local rules may ignore central coordination or broad incentives.

### 17. Lyapunov Function

- **Use when**: you need to know whether a system moves toward stability.
- **Core question**: Is there a quantity that consistently decreases or improves as the system evolves?
- **Watch for**: convergence, stability proofs, decreasing error, settling behavior.
- **Blind spot**: real systems may be disturbed before convergence matters.

### 18. Equilibrium

- **Use when**: the system appears to settle into a stable pattern.
- **Core question**: What state persists because no force or actor pushes it away?
- **Watch for**: stable prices, norms, routines, institutional arrangements.
- **Blind spot**: equilibrium can hide disequilibrium dynamics, power, and transition costs.

### 19. Markov Model

- **Use when**: actors or systems move among discrete states.
- **Core question**: What transition probabilities explain the long-run distribution of states?
- **Watch for**: user lifecycle states, health states, pipeline stages, churn states.
- **Blind spot**: memoryless transitions may ignore history and accumulated context.

### 20. Systems Dynamics

- **Use when**: stocks, flows, feedback, and delays shape behavior over time.
- **Core question**: What structure produces the observed time pattern?
- **Watch for**: backlogs, debt, inventory, trust, capacity, reinforcing and balancing loops.
- **Blind spot**: diagrams can become persuasive without being empirically tested.

### 21. Thresholds with Feedback

- **Use when**: small changes can trigger large shifts after a threshold is crossed.
- **Core question**: What tipping point changes the system's behavior?
- **Watch for**: adoption cascades, overload, morale collapse, bank runs, social movements.
- **Blind spot**: thresholds are often hard to estimate before they are crossed.

### 22. Spatial Choice

- **Use when**: location in physical, ideological, or product space matters.
- **Core question**: Where should actors position themselves relative to others and demand?
- **Watch for**: market positioning, political ideology, facility location, clustering.
- **Blind spot**: spatial simplifications can flatten multidimensional preferences.

### 23. Hedonic Choice

- **Use when**: value depends on bundles of attributes.
- **Core question**: Which attributes drive preference, price, or satisfaction?
- **Watch for**: product features, housing prices, compensation packages, service design.
- **Blind spot**: attribute models can miss social meaning and interactions among features.

### 24. Game Theory: Payoff Matrix

- **Use when**: each actor's outcome depends on choices made by others.
- **Core question**: What incentives does each actor face?
- **Watch for**: competition, negotiation, pricing, conflict, coordination.
- **Blind spot**: simplified payoffs may omit trust, norms, and repeated relationships.

### 25. Game Theory: Nash Equilibrium

- **Use when**: actors may settle into mutually reinforcing strategies.
- **Core question**: What strategy profile persists because no actor wants to deviate alone?
- **Watch for**: stalemates, stable competition, arms races, coordination traps.
- **Blind spot**: equilibrium does not explain how actors get there or whether it is desirable.

### 26. Game Theory: Sequential Games

- **Use when**: timing, commitment, threat, or first-mover advantage matters.
- **Core question**: How does the order of moves change strategic behavior?
- **Watch for**: negotiations, platform launches, policy commitments, escalation.
- **Blind spot**: sequential models require assumptions about what actors know and believe.

### 27. Cooperation Model

- **Use when**: actors can benefit from cooperation but may be tempted to defect.
- **Core question**: What conditions sustain cooperation?
- **Watch for**: repeated interaction, reciprocity, reputation, enforcement.
- **Blind spot**: cooperation models can underplay inequality and coercion.

### 28. Collective Action

- **Use when**: a group benefits from action but individuals have incentives to free ride.
- **Core question**: Why is the group failing to produce a shared good?
- **Watch for**: commons problems, public goods, volunteer burnout, governance gaps.
- **Blind spot**: not all inaction is free riding; capacity and legitimacy may also matter.

### 29. Mechanism Design

- **Use when**: rules can be designed to produce better behavior or information.
- **Core question**: What rules make desired actions incentive-compatible?
- **Watch for**: auctions, matching, governance, compensation, allocation systems.
- **Blind spot**: mechanisms can be gamed or rejected if they violate norms.

### 30. Signaling

- **Use when**: actors communicate hidden quality, intent, or type through costly signals.
- **Core question**: What signal is credible, and what is it trying to reveal?
- **Watch for**: credentials, warranties, branding, rituals, public commitments.
- **Blind spot**: signals can become performative theater detached from substance.

### 31. Learning and Multi-Armed Bandits

- **Use when**: the challenge is balancing exploration and exploitation.
- **Core question**: Should we keep using what works or try uncertain alternatives?
- **Watch for**: experiments, A/B tests, hiring bets, product bets, policy trials.
- **Blind spot**: bandit logic can ignore ethics, externalities, and long-term capability building.

### 32. Rugged Landscape

- **Use when**: performance depends on many interacting choices.
- **Core question**: Are we stuck on a local peak, and what kind of search can find better combinations?
- **Watch for**: innovation, strategy, organizational design, product configuration.
- **Blind spot**: landscapes are hard to observe; search metaphors can hide politics and implementation cost.
